# Design Context — zacculpan.com

## Design Context

### Users
Marketing and product professionals, startup founders, and operators — people actively building companies who are looking for frameworks and ideas they can apply. They're reading on breaks, between meetings, or during deep work sessions. They arrive via LinkedIn or word-of-mouth, often already curious about the author. They don't need to be convinced the topic matters — they need to feel the writing is worth their time.

### Brand Personality
**Warm, knowledgeable, curious.**

Not the self-promotional expert. Not the hustle-content guru. The person at the conference who gives you a genuinely useful answer, remembers your name, and makes you think differently about a problem you've been stuck on. Operator credibility without ego.

Voice tone: direct but not aggressive, thoughtful but not academic, personal but not overly casual.

### Emotional Goal
"This was helpful and informative and has inspired me with ideas for my business."

The benchmark: readers leave feeling *smarter and more capable*, not impressed by the author's credentials. Inspiration through usefulness — not through authority signals.

### References
- **Prof G Media** — opinionated, editorial, willing to take a clear position
- **Exit Five / Dave Gerhardt** — practitioner voice, B2B operator credibility, no-fluff
- **Lenny's Newsletter** — long-form, trusted, feels like getting advice from a smart friend
- **Humans of New York** — warmth, humanity, story as the primary vehicle
- **Heavyweight Podcast** — intimate, curious, emotionally resonant without being sentimental

Common thread: *real person, real thinking, real warmth.* These references have personality that bleeds through their medium.

### Anti-References
- **Alex Hormozi** — aggressive persuasion aesthetic, high-contrast urgency, "value bombs"
- **Ahrefs** — tool-heavy, SEO-dense, clinical information architecture
- **Aftershoot / Adobe** — polished but impersonal, corporate production quality without soul

### Aesthetic Direction
Simple with **splashes of delight**. The writing is the product — the design's job is to make reading feel like a pleasure, not to compete with the content. But "simple" should never mean boring or cold. Moments of warmth and surprise in interaction design (hover states, transitions, unexpected typography moments) reward the curious reader without demanding attention.

Light mode primary. The current dark/navy palette is good for OG images and accents, but the reading experience should feel like paper — warm, inviting, easy on the eyes.

### Design Principles

1. **Writing first, always.** Every design decision should make the writing easier to find and more pleasurable to read. If a visual element competes with the content, it loses.

2. **Warmth over polish.** Humanity beats production value here. A slightly imperfect moment of personality beats a slick, corporate-feeling component.

3. **Earn the delight.** Moments of surprise or joy should feel discovered, not performed. Subtle hover states, a typographic detail, an unexpected transition — not animations that demand applause.

4. **Operator credibility, not expert posturing.** Design signals should say "I've done this work" not "I am an authority." Concrete > Abstract. Specific > Generic.

5. **Build for the reader who hasn't decided yet.** The first-time visitor is still evaluating whether this person is worth following. Every page should answer: *why should I come back?*
