"Ecosystem" is becoming one of those words — like "community" before it — that gets applied to anything and means nothing.

Let me try to make it mean something specific.

## Community vs. Ecosystem: The Actual Difference

A community is a group of people who share something in common. An ecosystem is a group of people, companies, and tools that create value *for each other* — not just from a central hub.

Most SaaS "communities" are hub-and-spoke models. The company at the centre, users on the periphery. Content flows outward from the company. Value flows inward to the company.

A real ecosystem is different. Value flows in multiple directions. Your top customers are talking to each other, helping each other, building integrations, writing content, hosting events — independent of what you do.

## Why This Is the Only Durable Moat

Features can be copied in 90 days. Integrations take six months. An ecosystem takes years.

When your customers' success is genuinely intertwined with each other — when they rely on the network of other users, the shared knowledge base, the community of practitioners — switching becomes genuinely costly in a way that product differentiation alone never achieves.

This is how HubSpot built a category. How Salesforce became infrastructure. How Figma became the default design tool.

## Where to Start

If you're at a SaaS startup and you want to start building an ecosystem, the question to ask is: **who succeeds when your customers succeed?**

Agencies. Consultants. Complementary tools. Content creators in your space. Adjacent communities.

Your ecosystem starts by mapping those relationships and asking how you can create genuine value for each of them — not just extract value from them.

The companies that get this right in the next five years will be very hard to displace.
